Best Free AI Detector in 2026: An Honest Comparison

April 16, 2026 · 6 min read

There is no single best free AI detector in 2026. There are better and worse detectors for specific use cases, and the honest version of this comparison admits that upfront. This post is a head-to-head of the free options that are still worth using in 2026, the criteria that matter, and a recommendation based on the kind of work you are actually doing.

What to evaluate

Most review posts you will find online rank detectors by a single "accuracy" number, which is close to meaningless. The criteria that actually matter are:

  • Accuracy on current models (GPT-4, GPT-5, Claude 4, Gemini 2). Old benchmarks from 2023 are no longer representative.
  • False positive rate on real human writing, especially non-native English and academic prose.
  • Character limits and rate limiting on the free tier.
  • Multilingual support, if you work outside English.
  • Explainability. Does the tool show which sentences drove the score, or just hand you a number?
  • Speed and UX. Free should not mean painful.

The contenders

GPTZero (free tier)

The name everyone knows. Free tier caps at roughly 5,000 characters per check with an account. Solid accuracy on GPT-4 raw output, weaker on GPT-5 and on edited text. Shows sentence-level highlighting. Heavily optimized for English academic contexts. Slightly high false positive rate on formal writing.

Best for: students and teachers in English-language institutions who want the tool most instructors are familiar with.

ZeroGPT

Despite the confusing name, it is a different product. Free, no account needed, 15,000-character window per check. Fast. The UI is cluttered with ads. Accuracy on GPT-4 is comparable to GPTZero, sometimes a little lower on edited text. Occasionally returns 0% on clearly AI text, which suggests threshold issues.

Best for: quick checks of longer texts when you do not want to create an account.

Sapling AI Detector

Free up to 2,000 characters. Conservative tuning: lower recall, lower false positive rate. Returns a probability rather than a binary label. Sapling is the tool you use if you are worried about wrongly flagging a human writer. Weaker on creative writing.

Best for: editorial review where false positives are more costly than false negatives.

Copyleaks (free scan)

Limited free scan without subscription. Strong multilingual support, roughly 30 languages. Accuracy on English is comparable to GPTZero; accuracy on German, Spanish, and French is among the best available. Free scan is small and obviously designed to funnel you to paid.

Best for: content teams working in multiple languages who need occasional checks.

RealText

Our own tool, so take this with appropriate skepticism. RealText is free, requires no account, and supports six languages (English, Italian, Spanish, German, French, Portuguese). It reports perplexity and burstiness components separately so you can see why a text scored as it did, not just the final percentage. No character limit on the free tier, though very long texts are broken into segments. Accuracy on GPT-4 and Claude 4 is competitive with paid tools; on GPT-5 it sits around 60-70% recall like most competitors.

Best for: users who want transparency into what the detector is measuring, and for anyone working in a language other than English.

Honorable mentions

Writer.com's detector (small limit, embedded in their suite), Originality.ai demo (limited free use, strong on GPT-5), Content at Scale (mixed reviews, sometimes very aggressive). None of these make the main list for unrestricted free use. Our broader GPTZero alternatives article covers them in more detail.

Side-by-side summary

  • Longest free text window: ZeroGPT (15k chars), RealText (no hard limit).
  • Best multilingual: Copyleaks, RealText.
  • Lowest false positive rate: Sapling.
  • Most familiar to academic users: GPTZero.
  • Best explainability: RealText, GPTZero.
  • No account required: ZeroGPT, RealText.

How to use these together

Our recommendation is rarely a single tool. Run your text through two detectors with different strengths. If both say "likely AI" with high confidence, that is a real signal. If one says 90% AI and the other says 20%, the disagreement itself tells you the score is unreliable and you should not act on it. For the statistical reasons, see our article on how accurate AI detectors really are.

If you are a student worried about false positives, our self-check guide walks through the full workflow. If you are editing AI-assisted content, pair detection with the techniques in how to paraphrase AI text.

A recommendation by use case

  • Student self-checking an essay: GPTZero + RealText. GPTZero tells you what your instructor might see. RealText tells you why.
  • Editor reviewing a freelance submission: Sapling + Copyleaks. Both conservative; agreement is a strong signal.
  • Content marketer auditing a site: RealText + ZeroGPT for bulk checking, Originality demo for high-stakes pieces.
  • Non-English writing: Copyleaks or RealText. Avoid English-only tools.

The bigger picture

Free AI detectors in 2026 are good enough for first-pass screening and bad enough that you should never use one as the sole basis for a decision with real stakes. The best tool for a given task depends on what you care about. Pick two that complement each other, treat their output as one data point among several, and keep an eye on how the tools shift as models update.
