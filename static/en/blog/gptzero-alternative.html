GPTZero Alternatives: Free AI Detectors Worth Trying

April 16, 2026 · 6 min read

GPTZero was one of the first consumer-friendly AI detectors and is still the name most people recognize. It is not the only option, and for many users it is not the best one. If you are looking for a free GPTZero alternative, either because the free tier is too restrictive or because you want a second opinion, there are at least half a dozen serious competitors worth knowing.

Why use anything besides GPTZero?

GPTZero is solid on English GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 output, but three things push users to look elsewhere: the free tier caps at about 5,000 characters per check, multilingual support is thin, and the tool tends to over-flag clean academic writing. Running the same text through a second detector is also a good practice because no single engine is reliable enough to trust alone.

The alternatives, one by one

ZeroGPT

Despite the similar name, ZeroGPT is unrelated to GPTZero. It accepts up to 15,000 characters per check on the free tier and highlights flagged sentences. Accuracy on GPT-4 is roughly comparable to GPTZero on our tests, maybe slightly lower on heavily edited text. It is fast and requires no sign-up. The downside: the UI is cluttered and it sometimes reports 0% AI on obvious GPT output, which suggests its threshold tuning is aggressive toward false negatives.

Sapling AI Detector

Sapling's detector is free with a 2,000-character limit and offers an API. It tends to be one of the more conservative tools, meaning fewer false positives but also lower recall. Sapling is a good choice if you are worried about wrongly flagging human writing, especially in professional settings. It reports probabilities rather than a single label, which is useful for interpretation.

Copyleaks (free tier)

Copyleaks offers a limited free scan without a subscription. Their detector handles around 30 languages, which beats most competitors. On English text it performs in line with GPTZero. On Spanish and German it outperforms almost everyone. The catch: the free scan is small, and the full experience is gated behind a paid plan.

RealText

We obviously have a bias, but the positioning is straightforward. RealText is free, has no account requirement, handles English plus five other languages, and is designed to explain its score instead of just displaying it. It uses a perplexity-and-burstiness model combined with stylometric features. For the metrics behind the approach, see our post on perplexity and burstiness. You can also read our take on free AI content detectors more broadly.

Writer.com AI Content Detector

Writer's detector is free up to 1,500 characters and embedded in their broader writing suite. Accuracy is middling. Its main value is integration: if you already use Writer for content, running a quick check is one click. Standalone, it is not our top pick.

Originality.ai (demo)

Originality is a paid tool aimed at SEO agencies, but it exposes a small free demo that lets you try single-paragraph checks. Its scores on GPT-4 and Claude output are among the highest we have seen in independent testing, and it integrates fact-checking and plagiarism scanning. For anything beyond a quick trial, though, you need a subscription.

Quick comparison

  • Best for long texts: ZeroGPT (15,000-character window).
  • Best multilingual: Copyleaks (30+ languages), RealText (6 languages with full interface).
  • Lowest false positive rate: Sapling.
  • Best for quick honest scoring: RealText.
  • Best for SEO and publishing teams: Originality.ai, if you have a budget.

When to use more than one

Accuracy on any single detector is roughly 70-85% on realistic mixed samples, and scores vary wildly between tools on the same text. If the stakes are high (a publication decision, an academic integrity question, a hiring decision about an applicant's writing sample), run the text through at least two independent detectors. Agreement across tools is a much stronger signal than any single score. Our deep dive on detector accuracy explains why triangulation works.

A note on pricing

Every tool in this list has a free tier. All of them also have paid plans that raise character limits, add API access, or unlock team features. If you check fewer than a dozen texts per week, you should never need to pay. If you run a content team, the math changes quickly, and Originality or Copyleaks Enterprise become reasonable.

GPTZero remains a fine product. It just is not the only one, and for some workflows it is not the best. Try two or three of these alternatives on a sample you know the origin of, compare the scores, and pick whichever matches your intuition.
