How Accurate Are AI Detectors? The Honest Truth

April 16, 2026 · 6 min read

When someone asks how accurate are AI detectors, the answer they usually want is a single percentage. The real answer is messier. Accuracy depends on which model produced the text, whether a human edited it afterwards, how long the sample is, and what language the author typically writes in. A detector that scores 99% on a lab benchmark can drop below 65% on real-world content from your inbox.

The headline numbers and what they hide

Vendors love publishing a single accuracy figure. GPTZero claims around 85% on unedited GPT-generated text. Copyleaks advertises 99%. Originality.ai reports 96-98% on certain benchmarks. These numbers are not lies, but they are almost always measured under favorable conditions: long samples, a specific target model, no human editing, and English written by native speakers.

Independent evaluations tell a different story. A 2024 study at the University of Maryland tested seven commercial detectors on GPT-4 output that had been lightly edited by a human. The average true-positive rate fell to roughly 39%. In our own spot tests, ZeroGPT and Copyleaks agree on less than 70% of samples when the text is even moderately paraphrased. Precision, recall, and why both matter

Accuracy is not one number. Two separate metrics matter:

  • Precision: of all texts flagged as AI, how many really are AI. Low precision means innocent authors get accused.
  • Recall: of all AI texts in the wild, how many get caught. Low recall means students and spammers slip through.

Detectors tune a threshold that trades these off. Push precision to 99% and you miss half the AI content. Push recall to 99% and you flag 10-15% of genuinely human text. The F1 score, which balances both, is usually between 0.70 and 0.85 on realistic mixed datasets. That is solid, but it is not a lie detector.

Edited and hybrid text breaks everything

Pure ChatGPT output from 2023 was easy to spot. In 2026 most "AI content" is actually hybrid: a prompt produces a draft, a human rewrites paragraphs, moves sentences, adds their own examples. Once a human rewrites even 20% of the text, most detectors lose half their signal. Our tests mirror those of independent researchers investigating false positives: on lightly edited GPT-4 output, average true-positive rates sit around 55-65%.

False positives: the human cost

A 2023 Stanford study found that seven popular detectors flagged TOEFL essays by non-native English speakers as AI-generated 61% of the time. Native English essays from the same detectors: around 5%. That is a huge disparity, and it points to a structural problem: detectors learn that clean, predictable, slightly formal prose looks like AI, which also describes how many non-native writers and academic authors write.

Technical documentation, legal writing, and concise instructional content also trigger false positives at elevated rates. A detector that is 99% accurate in general can still be wrong one time in twenty, which is dangerous if you are using it to accuse a student.

What accuracy looks like by model

As of early 2026, rough detection rates look like this:

  • GPT-3.5 raw output: 85-95% detected across major tools.
  • GPT-4 raw: 70-85%.
  • GPT-5 raw with careful prompting: 45-65%.
  • Claude 3.5 / 4: 60-75% raw, lower when prompted to vary style.
  • Any of the above after 15 minutes of honest human editing: 30-55%.

You can validate these numbers on your own samples with a free tool like RealText or by cross-checking with a GPTZero alternative to see how different engines score the same passage.

How to interpret a detector score

Treat the percentage as one piece of evidence, not a verdict. A 95% AI score on a 300-word excerpt with no human context can reasonably trigger a conversation. The same score on a non-native English speaker writing a clean lab report should not end a career. If you are a student checking your own work, our guide to self-checking as a student explains how to read scores without panic.

The honest summary: modern AI detectors are useful signals, not proof. Used well, they catch careless AI dumping and flag texts that warrant a closer look. Used badly, they generate confident false accusations against the people least able to defend themselves.
